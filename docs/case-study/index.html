01 · THE PERSON

My mom.

A first-generation Korean immigrant who helps my dad manage franchise logistics. Some of the emails she handles are high-stakes: permits, bills, renewal notices, document requests.

She is not very comfortable with technology and faces a language barrier when reading emails in English. I'm usually the person she calls when she doesn't understand an email.

I'm not designing for a persona. I'm designing for one human being with a name and a face and a phone that buzzes me when she's stuck.

03 · RESEARCH

Where it
breaks down.

Five pain points emerged from the interview and from watching her open real emails on her laptop and phone. Translation alone doesn't fix any of them. They're about uncertainty, emotional load, and never having a "what do I do now" answer.

🔍

Can't find the ask

Frequently asks "what are they actually asking me to do?" after reading.

😮‍💨

Long emails = avoid

Long or formal emails get skimmed, then left unread "for later."

📝

No follow-up system

Relies on memory or handwritten reminders. Most things slip.

🔁

App-switching tax

Bounces between email, Translate, calendar, and notes. Friction kills follow-through.

😨

Fear of mistakes

Hesitates before replying because "I don't want to say the wrong thing."

04 · PLATFORM

Why a Chrome extension.

The problem happens inside email. A separate app would mean copy-paste, context-switch, and another tool to learn. Three things that already kill her follow-through.

Not a mobile app: she reads email on both phone and laptop, and the deciding-what-to-do problem isn't mobile-specific.

Not a separate translation tool: Google Translate already gives her words. Words aren't her problem. Purpose and next steps are.

Not a chatbot: moving the solution off email adds steps instead of removing them.

Why a Chrome extension: it lives directly on top of Gmail. Same place she's already stuck.

05 · THE BUILD
The Build · Overview

One panel, four tabs,
thirteen features.

A floating panel that reads the live Gmail email she has open, classifies it, and answers what does this mean, what should I do, when, and is it safe? in the language she trusts.

  • Floating · draggable · resizable · minimizable
  • Four reading levels: Korean · English · Bilingual · Simple English
  • Real Gmail DOM reading, no copy-paste
  • Reminders persist in localStorage between sessions

The Build · Feature

"Show me where" +
walkthrough mode.

The biggest insight from testing wasn't comprehension. It was hesitation. She'd understand what to do but freeze at "which thing do I click?"

  • Click "Show me where" → blue spotlight pulses on the exact element in the email
  • "Start Walkthrough" runs the whole task step-by-step
  • Tooltip lives to the left of the panel, never covers the email
  • "I Need More Help" escalates to 5 calmer modes

The Build · Feature

Reminders that
actually have dates.

From the research: "I either leave the tab open or mark it unread and hope I remember." That's not a reminder system. That's a coping mechanism.

  • Inline picker on every date: Tomorrow · Next Week · Custom
  • Center groups them: Overdue · Today · This Week · Upcoming
  • Countdown labels: "⚠ Overdue" · "Due in 3 days"
  • Snooze 1 day · restart the walkthrough from the reminder

06 · FAILURES
Failure 01 · Too minimal

"Ship v1 first,
expand later".

The first Chrome extension was a stripped-down version of the simulation: just a summary card and a 2-option escalation. No glossary, no date picker, no case memory, no reminder grouping.

Why it failed:

The simulation she'd already seen in First Contact had all those features. Installing this on her real Gmail would have given her a worse tool than the demo. The whole point, "evidence that you helped someone," would have collapsed.

→ Rebuilt to full feature parity (~1700 lines).

Failure 02 · Covered the email

The Grammarly
sidebar fallacy.

Second version pinned the panel to the right edge of Gmail, full height. The reasoning: "this matches Grammarly and Gmail's own side panels."

Why it failed:

Mom's Gmail layout already has things on the right (Calendar, Tasks). A full-height fixed panel literally covered the email she was trying to read. The simulation she'd seen had a floating window. Switching to a sidebar made it feel like a different product and broke the trust she'd built.

→ Rebuilt as floating window with 8 resize handles + minimize-to-pill.

06 · FAILURES
Failure 03 · Reminders with no when

"Saved!" isn't
a reminder.

"Save this email as a reminder" auto-saved with no date prompt. One click, a toast appeared, done. AI defended this as "cleaner UX, mom shouldn't have to think about when."

Why it failed:

The whole reason she misses things isn't that she doesn't want to set reminders. It's that "I'll deal with this later" never has a when attached, so later never comes. A reminder with no date lives in "Upcoming" forever. Functionally identical to no reminder.

→ Rebuilt the button to open the same picker as the date cards.

07 · ITERATION

What testing changed.

Every observation from First Contact mapped to a specific change in the build.

She immediately understood simplified action sections
→
Kept simplified mode but narrowed it: only hides dense list-style cards, not the supportive sections (Glossary, Case Memory)
Calmer, conversational language worked
→
Korean tone locked to 해요체, polite but not stiff. Emoji used sparingly to signal type, not decorate
Reminder feature felt intuitive without explanation
→
Date picker promoted from a hidden secondary action to a prominent button on every detected date + a "Save email as reminder" button on every checklist
Wanted a clearer place to revisit saved reminders
→
Built the Reminder Center: filter pills (All / Urgent / Renewals / Appointments / Bills), grouped by Overdue / Today / This Week / Upcoming with countdown labels and snooze
Asked for more guidance when she felt unsure
→
Added "I Need More Help" escalation flow with 5 modes (cant-find · slower · scared · dont-understand · human) + enhanced guide highlight with bouncing arrow

08 · WHAT I LEARNED
What I learned
Hypothetical users let you off the hook. A real person you know doesn't. She just doesn't click the button you spent two hours on, and her face tells you why before she can articulate it.
Designing for one person didn't make the product narrower. It made it sharper. The features that solve mom's specific problem are the same ones that would help anyone else navigating an unfamiliar language inside an unfamiliar bureaucracy. The opposite path, designing for "all immigrant users," would have produced a vaguer, more compromised tool.
